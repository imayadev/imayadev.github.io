Can We Grow Organs in the Lab?

By Maya A., Year 12 · 16 October 2025 · ~4 min read

In the last decade, 3D bioprinting has moved from an early idea to a serious area of biomedical research. It brings together biology, materials science and engineering to create living structures from cells. Instead of printing with plastic or metal, scientists use a cell mixture called bioink, combined with a gel that gives support and nutrients. During a talk I gave at the Health Professions Society, I explained how this process works in practice and why it matters for medicine.

The work starts with a digital model of the tissue or organ that researchers want to recreate. A printer deposits layers of bioink that follow the model, similar to how a normal 3D printer builds an object layer by layer. Over time, the printed cells can grow and connect to form functioning tissue. Teams have already printed pieces of cartilage and skin, and small sections of heart muscle that can contract. These are not full organs, but they show that the basic method is sound.

One of the hardest problems is building a stable network of blood vessels. Cells need oxygen and nutrients, so printed tissue will not survive without some kind of circulation. Some groups print tiny channels that act like capillaries, while others seed the structure with stem cells that can develop into vessels. There are also questions about mechanical strength and cell arrangement, because each tissue has its own structure and chemical signals that guide how it behaves.

In my presentation, I focused on drug testing and transplants. Bioprinted organoids, which are miniature versions of organs, already help researchers study disease and test treatments with more accuracy than animal models. Looking further ahead, printing replacement organs from a patient’s own cells could lower the chance of rejection and reduce waiting lists for donors.

Printing a full-sized, fully working organ will take many more years of research. Even so, the progress is significant and steady. Bioprinting changes how we think about the body because it treats organs as systems that can be rebuilt using cells, materials and careful design. It combines creativity with precision and it aims at a clear goal in medicine: repairing damaged tissue and restoring function in a safe and reliable way.
